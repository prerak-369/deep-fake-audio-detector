Financial Industry — Deepfake Audio Fraud Patterns and Response Frameworks

SCENARIO 1: CEO / CFO FRAUD — WIRE TRANSFER AUTHORISATION
An attacker clones a senior executive's voice and submits a recording authorising a large wire transfer to an external account. Finance staff, believing the voice to be authentic, processes the transfer. This is the most prevalent deepfake fraud vector in financial services.
Real-world precedent: A UK energy company lost £201,000 in 2019 when attackers used AI voice cloning to impersonate the CEO and authorise a transfer. FBI IC3 estimates Business Email Compromise (including voice variants) caused $2.9 billion in losses in 2023.

DETECTION SIGNATURE: High confidence deepfake score, submitter is claiming executive authority, transaction value is unusually large, request is time-pressured ("urgent", "end of day").

RESPONSE: HALT transaction immediately. Verify executive identity through a pre-established secondary channel (call the executive's known mobile number directly). File a SAR if transfer was initiated.

SCENARIO 2: AUDIT EVIDENCE TAMPERING
A bad actor submits a deepfake audio recording as evidence during an internal or external audit — misrepresenting what was said in a meeting or call. This undermines audit integrity and may constitute obstruction of justice or securities fraud.

DETECTION SIGNATURE: Audio submitted specifically in response to an audit request, content conveniently supports a disputed position, submitter has had prior compliance issues.

RESPONSE: Treat as potential obstruction. Preserve original file with cryptographic hash. Notify external auditors immediately. Escalate to General Counsel.

SCENARIO 3: IDENTITY FRAUD IN HIRING
Deepfake audio used during a remote interview to misrepresent the candidate's identity, particularly for roles with access to sensitive financial systems. The real person (whose voice was cloned) may be unaware their voice was used.

SCENARIO 4: COORDINATED CAMPAIGN INDICATORS
Multiple deepfake submissions targeting the same department within a short time window indicate an organised threat actor. Key signals:
- 3+ deepfake attempts within 30 days targeting same department
- Similar audio characteristics across incidents (same TTS engine signature)
- Escalating transaction values across attempts
- Submitter accounts created recently or with unusual access patterns

RESPONSE TO COORDINATED ATTACKS: Treat as an Advanced Persistent Threat (APT). Engage Information Security immediately. Consider law enforcement referral. Freeze all pending authorisations from the targeted department pending enhanced review.
