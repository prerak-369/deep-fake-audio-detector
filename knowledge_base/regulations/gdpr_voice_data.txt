GDPR — Voice Data Processing and Deepfake Incident Obligations

ARTICLE 5 — PRINCIPLES OF DATA PROCESSING
Voice recordings constitute biometric data under GDPR when used for identification purposes. Processing must be lawful, fair, and transparent. A deepfake audio that impersonates a real individual without consent violates their biometric data rights under Article 9 (special category data).

ARTICLE 33 — NOTIFICATION OF PERSONAL DATA BREACH (72-HOUR RULE)
If a deepfake audio incident results in the unauthorised processing of a real person's voice (i.e., their voice was cloned without consent), this constitutes a personal data breach. The data controller must notify the competent supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach. Failure to notify within 72 hours requires a documented justification for the delay.

ARTICLE 34 — COMMUNICATION TO DATA SUBJECTS
If the breach is likely to result in high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons (e.g., the individual whose voice was cloned may suffer financial or reputational harm, or identity fraud), the affected individual must be notified without undue delay. Notification must describe the nature of the breach, contact details of the DPO, likely consequences, and measures taken.

ARTICLE 83 — PENALTIES
Violations of Article 5 (data processing principles) or Article 9 (special category data): up to €20 million or 4% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher.
Violations of Article 33/34 (breach notification): up to €10 million or 2% of annual global turnover.

KEY OBLIGATIONS FOR DEEPFAKE DETECTION SYSTEMS:
Deepfake detection systems processing voice data must comply with data minimisation (Article 5(1)(c)) — do not retain raw audio longer than necessary for the audit purpose. A Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) under Article 35 is likely required when deploying voice authentication systems at scale.
